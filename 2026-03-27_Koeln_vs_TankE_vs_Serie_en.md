# Comparison: Cologne vs. Series Launch vs. TankE

- `2026`: Rheinmetall und TankE wollen Ladebordsteine in die Staedte bringen

## Short Conclusion

Across all three years, the debate remains mostly skeptical, but its focus shifts. In 2023, the dominant question is whether curbside chargers are ergonomically and urbanistically a good idea at all. In 2025, the same criticism is not removed by the series launch, but it is now confronted with a more concrete real-world argument: a supposedly successful test year. In 2026, the discussion becomes more practical again while also taking on a stronger political and symbolic dimension, not least because Rheinmetall is involved.

## 1. What stays constant?

The same core doubts appear again and again in all three threads:

- ground-level handling is consistently seen as inconvenient
- dirt, rain, snow, ice, and general street conditions are treated as the main burden on the concept
- real-world parking behavior at the curb is considered harder than any product demo suggests
- many commenters accept the need for urban charging, but not automatically this specific physical format

So what remains constant is not opposition to charging infrastructure itself, but skepticism toward the curb as the human interface.

## 2. What defines the 2023 Cologne thread?

The Cologne thread is the broadest and most fundamental debate. Here, the curb charger is discussed primarily as a concept problem.

Characteristic features:

- ergonomics is the strongest topic
- the debate expands strongly into urban traffic, parking space, and mobility policy
- many alternative proposals appear, such as classic charging poles, charging hubs, or other visible systems
- approval is usually limited to “interesting, but please in a different and better form”

The Cologne thread is therefore the phase of broad conceptual criticism.

## 3. What changes in the 2025 series-launch thread?

The thread about the successful test year shifts the focus. The issue is no longer only whether the idea sounds plausible, but how much weight actual testing should carry against obvious everyday concerns.

Characteristic features:

- weather, dirt, and robustness remain the strongest topic
- the series launch acts as a counterargument to blanket rejection
- nevertheless, comfort, sidewalk use, and parking questions remain largely unchanged
- the discussion becomes more concrete: bollards, distance to the car, installation sites, heavy rain, driving over the unit

The series thread is therefore the phase of plausibility testing: “If it has already been tested, which criticisms still remain serious?”

## 4. What defines the 2026 TankE/Rheinmetall thread?

In 2026, the debate shifts back more strongly into the urban and political space. Practical everyday criticism remains dominant, but it is now amplified by the manufacturer question and the symbolism of Rheinmetall.

Characteristic features:

- parking, curb placement, and streetscape are the most frequent topics
- ergonomics and weather remain very strong
- positive voices usually argue from field tests or a narrower use case
- Rheinmetall creates its own side thread of irony, symbolism, and political perception

The 2026 thread is therefore the phase of public framing: less of a pure technology debate, more of a mix of everyday use, infrastructure, and actor perception.

## 6. How does the tone change?

The tone does not become clearly more positive. What changes is mainly the form of skepticism:

- `2023`: “The concept seems flawed at its core, or at least poorly thought through.”
- `2025`: “Even after a test year, the same everyday-use problems are still visible.”
- `2026`: “The concept may work in niches, but in normal urban life many people are still not convinced by its usability, maintenance, and installation logic.”

In other words: the debate moves from fundamental rejection to testing-oriented skepticism and then to conditional acceptance of limited use cases.

## 7. Shared conclusion

Taken together, all three threads show a remarkably stable pattern. Hardly anyone disputes the need for charging infrastructure for people without a private wallbox. The real point of conflict is whether a ground-level charging point at the curb is the right human-machine interface for that need. The further the concept moves from prototype to test to deployment, the less the debate focuses on the abstract “whether” and the more it focuses on trust, maintenance reality, installation context, and limited but plausible use cases.

## 8. Condensed management statement

From 2023 to 2026, the core criticism of curbside chargers remains strikingly stable: ergonomics, dirt/weather exposure, and parking reality dominate throughout. The 2025 series launch and the 2026 expansion do not remove the pressure to justify the concept; they only change the shape of the debate. Acceptance appears mainly where curbside chargers are understood as a special-purpose neighborhood solution or as a testable complement to other forms of charging. Broad enthusiasm for them as a standard public-charging model is not visible in any of the three threads.
